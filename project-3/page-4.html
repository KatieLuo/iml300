Good Representation (for now)

Hover over the films below to see why they are good representation. Although many of these films are flawed in its own ways, they are‚Äîfor the most part‚Äîa step in the right direction.

Note: It was especially difficult finding media that represents the Pacific Islander community well. This is just another reflection of the lack of diversity within an already representation-bare industry.

"Saving Face" is about a Chinese immigrant mother and daugter who navigate their relationship with each other and love. Director Alice Wu creates deep and dynamic characters who showcase authentic first and second generation Chinese-American experiences. The characters must learn to deal with social standing in the highly judgemental Chinese community, reputation, dignity, and honor.

Hulu's "The Mindy Project" is about an Indian-American ob-gyn, Mindy Lahiri, who naviagtes her career life and romantic relationships. This film redirects South Asian women roles in film. While many shows such as "Master of None" and "The Night Of" cast South Asian women as undesirable side-characters, "The Mindy Project" gives an Indian-American woman the proper amount of space to be nuanced and interesting.

"Turning Red," directed by Domee Shi, tells a compelling story that anyone from any background can relate to. Although there are several valid criticisms, Shi uses universal emotions such as healing from generational trauma to allow the audience to empathize with specific communities.

"Never Have I Ever" is a classic, teen coming-of-age show about a South Asian high schooler who navigates the melodramas of school and growing up while better undersatnding her Indian-American identity. This show has been praised by the South Asian American community for making them and their families feel seen and understood‚Äî a wonderful step in the right direction.

While "Kim's Convenience" has had it's fair share of criticisms for a racist writers' room, it still hits the spot for a heartwarming portrayal of a Korean-Canadian family who runs a small corner store. It is "quietly revolutionary" for being one of the most successful mainstream Asian immigrant stories.

"Boy" is about a 11-year-old Maori boy whos world is centered around Michael Jackson's "Thriller" video. Although this movie is not necessarily a Pacific-Islander-American film, it still highlights a Pacific-Islander story and their relationship with American culture. Boy, the main character, imagines his absent father as a version of Michael Jackson. The film elegantly explores the complexity of family dysfunction and hero worship.

"Raya and The Last Dragon" introduces Disney's first Southeast Asian princess. The animated film, set in the fantastical world of Kumandra, in inspired by Southeast Asian cultures. The film was co-written by Vietnamese-American screenwriter Qui Nguyen and Malaysian screenwriter Adele Lim. This film allows for Southeast representation behind and on the screen. While the film references several cultures and traditions by casting a wider net, combining multiple Southeast Asian countries' cultures into one is questionable.

"The Half Of It" highlights the intersectionality of the female, queer, Asian American youth. This film is director Alice Wu's second film‚Äî both featuring characters of similar backgorunds. Although the premise of both her films are fairly similar on the surface level, both tell different nuanced stories. In "The Half Of It," highschooler, Ellie Chu, experiences heart break and self-discovery. Wu's films are so well done because she puts her own life into her work. The protagonists are imperfect, yet lovable people.

"The Green Knight," casted by Dev Patel, created room for good representation through its diverse cast. The film does not directly speak on its diversity, however, the casting choices have have spoken volumes for the Southeast Asian community. Rarely do we see blockbuster of this scale cast Asian characters is a film inspired by the sixth-century British Isles.

"Sound of Metal" represents the deaf and South Asian communities in modern cinema. Darius Marder's film follows a flawed musician, Riz Ahmed, who struggles with the loss of his hearing. The film is compassionate and multi-dimensional; Ahmed does not address his racial ties, but he is a wonderfully written character that is usually saved for white actors.

"Minari" offers thoughtful insight into the Korean immigrant experience. The film explores realism through highlighting the highly-intentional, specific, and authentic moments of director Lee Isaac Chung's childhood in rural Arkansas in the 1980s. The characters battle intergenerational conflicts and the "American Dream." "Minari" has been claimed to be the perfect representation of openness and diversity in Hollywood.

"Moana" has become a classic animated film across the world. The film displays good representation through its themes, characters, and overall narrative. Maui is a Polynesian mythology that holds great cultural significance and symbolism for many Pacific Islanders. The animated film has been claimed to represent "true Polynesian strength, determination, and pride."
